How to Teach Your Child About Prayer:
5 Ways to Make It Stick

Your child rushes through mealtime prayers so fast the words blur together. You've tried everything — here's what actually works.

JV
By Jandre van der Walt
📅 May 28, 2025
⏱️ 7 min read
👪 For Parents
🙏

"Your eight-year-old rushes through mealtime prayers so quickly the words blur together. You've taught her repeatedly, yet the lessons don't seem to stick."

Here's the reality most parenting books won't tell you: prayer isn't something you teach kids once and check off the list. Prayer is a relationship, and like any relationship, it grows through practice, invitation, and genuine connection — not a single Sunday morning lesson.

The good news? You don't need a theology degree or a perfect quiet time routine. You just need a few strategies that meet your child where they are. Here are five that actually work.

The Real Problem With How We Teach Prayer

Traditional approaches typically involve sitting children down to explain prayer components — Adoration, Confession, Thanksgiving, Supplication. The acronyms are memorable. The results, often, are not.

Children who are taught prayer as a formula tend to do exactly that: follow the formula. They go through the motions without genuine engagement. They say the words, but their hearts aren't in the room.

The issue isn't the technique. It's the context. Children learn to pray by actually praying — by observing how the adults in their lives talk to God, and by witnessing prayer's impact on real, everyday circumstances they care about.

What Prayer Actually Is (And How to Explain It Simply)

Before you can teach your child how to pray, help them understand what prayer actually is. The simplest and most powerful explanation you can give?

"Prayer is just talking to God like you talk to me."

Children often believe prayer requires special, formal, or archaic language — that God only responds to the "right" words said in the "right" tone. This belief quietly crushes their prayer lives before they even begin.

✅ Give Your Child Permission to Know:
  • Prayer can happen anywhere — in the car, at school, in bed, on the playground
  • Any topic works — fears, friendships, gratitude, dreams, even small everyday things
  • Personal, everyday language is not just allowed — it's welcomed
  • Simple expressions like "Help me, God" or "Thank you" are real, complete prayers

5 Strategies That Actually Work

Strategy 1

Model Prayer Out Loud — Every Day

"Your child will pray the way she hears you pray." This is probably the single most powerful thing in this article. Children don't learn to pray from instruction — they learn from imitation. When they hear you talk honestly to God about your own day, your own fears, your own gratitude, they absorb a living, breathing model of what real prayer looks like.

This means praying out loud in ordinary moments: expressing work anxiety in the car on the way to school, thanking God for a beautiful sunset, asking for patience in the middle of a hard day. It doesn't have to be long. It doesn't have to be eloquent. It just has to be real.

Strategy 2

Start With What They Actually Care About

Rather than introducing scripture-based prayer formats right away, begin with topics that are meaningful to your child right now — a friendship conflict, a test they're worried about, their football game on Saturday, a fear of the dark. When children experience prayer addressing their actual circumstances, and then witness God respond (in any of the countless ways He does), they discover that prayer isn't just a ritual. It's relevant.

Strategy 3

Teach the "CHAT" Prayer Framework

When your child is ready for a little more structure, the CHAT framework offers guidance without rigidity. The key is that children don't need to follow it in strict order — it's a starting point, not a script.

C
Confession Acknowledging mistakes and asking for forgiveness — teaches honesty with God.
H
Honoring God Expressing who God is — His love, His power, His goodness.
A
Asking Making requests — for themselves, for others, for the world.
T
Thanksgiving Expressing gratitude — the single fastest way to shift a child's attitude.
Strategy 4

Create a "Prayer Jar" or "Prayer Wall"

Visual tools are powerful for concrete thinkers — and most children under twelve think very concretely. Write prayer requests on paper slips and store them in a jar. Keep a visible board on the fridge or bedroom wall. Mark answers when they come. The accumulation of answered prayers becomes one of the most faith-building experiences a child can have. They can see, with their own eyes, that prayer does something.

Strategy 5

Pray With Her — Not Just Have Her Pray Alone

There's a significant difference between telling a child to pray and praying alongside them. Joint prayer creates spiritual intimacy. Bedtime prayers, mealtime discussions, and moments of crisis are all rich opportunities for shared spiritual connection — before independent prayer practice can develop.

Overcoming the Most Common Obstacles

👶 "They're too young to understand"

Basic concepts are enough — God loves us, God listens, God wants us to talk to Him. Even a toddler can understand that someone important loves them and wants to hear from them.

😴 "They get bored during prayer time"

Reconnect by addressing something they currently care about. Boredom in prayer almost always means the prayer isn't relevant to their actual life right now.

🙅 "They refuse to participate"

Model prayer without demanding participation. Pray for them privately. Don't turn prayer into a battle — that association will last far longer than one difficult season.

😬 "I'm not comfortable praying myself"

Your personal prayer development and your child's are more connected than you think. Growing together is not a weakness — it's one of the most meaningful things you can do as a parent.

🎯

The Real Goal

The objective isn't flawless prayer performance. It's raising a child who sees God as accessible, responsive, and genuinely involved in her real life. When prayer transforms from task to conversation — addressing real circumstances with a God who actually shows up — it becomes a sustainable, life-long practice rooted in authentic relationship.

That's the prayer life every parent dreams of for their child. And it starts not with a curriculum, but with you — praying out loud, right now, about the things that actually matter today.
